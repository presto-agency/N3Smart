Loren Ipsum

In Lillestrøm municipality, they have used technology and sensor support for several years. With sensors in carpets, doors and dispensers in some selected buildings, the cleaners only need to top up or clean when needed.

Traditional cleaning is structured in such a way that you have to perform tasks after a certain frequency, regardless of whether the need is there or not. We simply work smarter, so that we can maintain the quality of the cleaning according to the standard we set.

Previously, it was common to carry out cleaning at fixed times at fixed intervals. The technology has opened up new possibilities for demand-driven cleaning. The emergence of interconnected devices and equipment that is capable of generating data and communicating with each other over the Internet ("Internet of Things", IoT), gives cleaning staff access to completely new information and insights into usage patterns, cleaning routines and product consumption.

The nice thing about sensors is that they give you facts. It's one thing to believe, but it's another thing to know, and you get to show it with sensors, says Dalbo Nygaard.

Loren Ipsum

Dalbo Nygaard exemplifies with the door counters that are installed in the City Hall in Lillestrøm, among other places. With the help of these, it is possible to see how much a given floor or room has been in use, and control the cleaning accordingly.

- A meeting room that has not been in use one day, there is no point in cleaning. While in those days there have been 200 pieces there, there may be a need for cleaning several times. The sensors give us this insight, and we have benefited greatly from that, says Nygaard.
